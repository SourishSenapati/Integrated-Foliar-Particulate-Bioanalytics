# Literature Review Matrix: Comparison of Plant Biomonitors for PM Monitoring

This matrix compiles key data from recent (2024-2026) and foundational studies on the particulate matter (PM) monitoring potential of various plant species.

---

## 1. Primary Review: Indirect Soil Monitoring via Leaf Spectra

**Title:** Rapid monitoring of soil heavy metals in urban road greenbelts using leaf spectral characteristic
**Authors:** Qi, Y., Dong, Z., Pu, B., Yang, Q., & Wei, H.
**Year:** 2026
**Journal:** Journal of Hazardous Materials, 506, 141565.
**DOI:** [10.1016/j.jhazmat.2026.141565](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jhazmat.2026.141565)

**Background:**
Investigates the potential of using leaf hyperspectral reflectance as a proxy for monitoring soil heavy metal (HM) concentrations (Cd, Cr, Cu, Ni, Pb, Zn) in complex urban greenbelts.

**Study Area & Sampling:**
Chongqing, China. Leaf and soil samples were collected from *Ficus concinna* and *Loropetalum chinense* var. *rubrum* across urban road networks.

**Material & Method:**
Pairs of soil/leaf samples analyzed via hyperspectral sensors (350-2500 nm). Data preprocessed with Savitzky-Golay (SG), First/Second Derivatives (FD/SD), and Multiplicative Scatter Correction (MSC). Models: Partial Least Squares (PLS) and Random Forest (RF).

**Result & Discussions:**
*F. concinna* proved superior for spectral inversion, with PLS models achieving $R^2 > 0.74$ for all six heavy metals. Soil Cd showed the highest enrichment (3.2x background). The study confirms that physiological stress from soil toxins manifests in detectable leaf spectral signatures.

**Conclusion:**
Leaf spectral characteristics offer a high-efficiency, non-destructive method for rapid assessment of soil HM pollution at an urban scale.

## 2. Biochemical Interaction: PM-Induced Wax Degradation

**Title:** Historical Pollution Exposure Impacts on PM2.5 Dry Deposition and Physiological Responses in Urban Trees
**Authors:** Liu, R., Wang, M., Chen, S., Zhang, J., Jin, X., Ren, Y., & Chen, J.
**Year:** 2024
**Journal:** Forests, 15(9), 1614.
**DOI:** [10.3390/f15091614](https://doi.org/10.3390/f15091614)

**Background:**
Examines how long-term exposure to urban pollutants alters the physical structure and chemical integrity of the leaf cuticle, specifically the epicuticular wax layer.

**Study Area & Sampling:**
Subtropical urban forest gradients (China). Focused on the cumulative impact of PM2.5 on tree physiological health.

**Material & Method:**
Foliar PM quantification combined with Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) of wax structures. Physiological markers included Chlorophyll Fluorescence ($F_v/F_m$) and stomatal conductance.

**Result & Discussions:**
Prolonged PM exposure causes a physical "fusion" or "erosion" of crystalline wax structures. This biochemical degradation reduces the leaf's water-repellency and increases vulnerability to secondary stresses. It also correlates with a significant decline in photosynthetic efficiency (lower $F_v/F_m$).

**Conclusion:**
Epicuticular wax is not just a passive trap for PM but a dynamic biochemical surface that degrades under chronic pollution, serving as a vital indicator of long-term plant health.

## 3. Global Synthesis: Mechanism of PM Retention

**Title:** Analysing the Impact of Atmospheric Particulate Matter Deposition on Major Soil and Plants Attributes: A Review
**Authors:** Singh, R., & Singh, H.
**Year:** 2025
**Journal:** Water, Air, & Soil Pollution, 236(814).
**DOI:** [10.1007/s11270-025-08472-3](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11270-025-08472-3)

**Background:**
A comprehensive review of the physical and chemical impacts of PM on the soil-plant continuum, transitioning from surface deposition to systemic toxicity.

**Study Area & Sampling:**
Literature meta-analysis covering diverse climatic zones (Tropical to Temperate).

**Material & Method:**
Systematic review of indices like APTI (Air Pollution Tolerance Index) and physical accumulation coefficients across various plant habits.

**Result & Discussions:**
Confirms that alkaline dusts (rich in Ca/Mg oxides) accelerate the chemical weathering of leaf waxes. The review identifies a "threshold of saturation" where excessive PM layers inhibit gas exchange and transpiration, leading to premature leaf senescence.

**Conclusion:**
The efficacy of a plant as a biomonitor depends on its balanced capacity to retain PM while resisting the biochemical degradation of its own protective layers.

## 4. Foundational Comparison: Species Efficiency

**Title:** Plant species differences in particulate matter accumulation on leaf surfaces
**Authors:** Sæbø, A., Popek, R., et al.
**Year:** 2012
**Journal:** Science of the Total Environment, 427, 347-354.
**DOI:** [10.1016/j.scitotenv.2012.03.084](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.scitotenv.2012.03.084)

**Background:**
Evaluates the physical PM-trapping potential of 47 different tree and shrub species.

**Study Area & Sampling:**
Urban settings in Norway and Poland.

**Material & Method:**
Quantification of PM sizes (PM10, PM2.5, PM0.2) on surface vs. in-wax layers.

**Result & Discussions:**
Found a 15-fold difference in efficiency between top performers (*Pinus mugo*) and low performers (*Tilia cordata*). High trichome density and thick wax were the primary physical drivers of capture.

**Conclusion:**
Species selection is the single most important factor for urban "green lung" design.
